This is the 0th post of my blog series “Simple QKD”. In this series, I aim to introduce Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) in simple English at an undergraduate level.

I’m by no means anywhere near being an “expert” in this topic. QKD is one of my research interests, and a topic I’m actively reading about and learning. The objective of this blog is to introduce basic concepts to readers at a ~1st/2nd year undergraduate level, and to refer interested readers to more relevant and more advanced-level materials. Writing these blogs would also be a learning process for myself. They will be by no means error-free. If you find any mistakes, or would like to give me any suggestions, please contact me via email.

In this 0th post of the series, I’d like to start by talking about myself and why I’m interested in this topic.

(Health Warning: This is a post summarising my research experiences in quantum information, of which I might take bits and pieces and put in my future job/PhD application forms. It may not be the most interesting piece of reading material if you are not looking to employ me ;P)

Initial interest

I loved my Quantum Mechanics course in second year.

It was so different from the classical physics I got used to. The maths was second most elegant in all physics I’ve learned (No.1 being Relativity); The probabilistic nature makes it mysterious; And so many questions unanswered by the Copenhagen interpretation leave so many possibilities open.

In third year I chose to do a Research Review. Professor Mike Payne at Cavendish offered to supervise quantum information topics. It was “love at first sight” when he explained the history of this subject in the simplest way:

The older generation of scientists scratched their heads and spent their lifetime working on a “correct” interpretation of quantum mechanics, while the younger generation asked themselves, “Ok, quantum physics is wrong, but what can we do with it?” [^1]. And thus quantum information was born, in the 70s/80s.

The pragmatism behind this story attracted me immediately. Science can advance our life, even if it’s “imperfect” science. That’s the story of all science: Galilean physics, Newtonian physics, Einstein physics, chemistry, biology etc. And it is the purpose of “usefulness” that should motivate us scientists in moving forward [^2].

A second, much more obvious actually, motivation for me was the claim of “quantum supremacy” [^3]by Google. Every major global power (US, China, Europe, UK, Japan etc.) are also building their heavily funded quantum projects. I simply don’t want to miss the party.

What I’ve done

I’m still a newcomer to this party, just like a fresher awkwardly making their way around in college, and passionately going to every lecture they could find. I’d like to call this section “my quantum road”, but that would be a wild exaggeration of my baby steps so far.

Research Review (2019.11-2020.05)

The first thing I did was a research review project on QKD under Prof Payne. I read about 30 papers about this topic, and produced a 3000-word review “Quantum Key Distribution and its Practical Implementations”.

This review, my first ever serious attempt at paper reading and literature reviewing, was far from perfect. It lacked quantitative details, critical analysis and sacrificed too much depth for broadness. But I’m still very proud of it. It is the first academic-journal style report I’ve ever produced, and the experience taught me so much: How to read a paper, how to summarise a paper, how to search for papers, and how to compile a review etc. The project also helped me understand the basics of QKD and single photon technologies [^4].

PM QKD Project (2020.06-2020.10)

The second thing I did was a summer research project with Prof. Xiongfeng Ma on phase-matching (PM) QKD protocol. I did theoretical investigations on various setups of PM QKD aiming to improve its rate-loss performance.

In the first month on the project, I did nothing but read. Any thoughts of “I know QKD” was completely gone by the end of first week.

In the second month of the project, I attempted to calculate the rate-loss performance for various setups. Any thoughts of “I know quantum physics” was completely gone by the end of first week (sounds familiar eh). Nothing teaches physics better than attempting a real question.

During the project I had weekly meetings with Prof Ma’s group. Typically I would do a 1-hour presentation on my progress, and we would discuss what I did correctly, what I did wrong, and what I should do the next week.

The project taught me an important lesson in dealing with failures. During the project, it almost seemed as if hope and despair came on alternate weeks: One week the calculations would show very promising potential, another week they would show the opposite. And every time I found an error in my calculations, it was a week’s effort in vain. But gradually I learned to deal with such setbacks: They are common in research, and the most important thing is to analyse the cause, mentally recover, and get back to work.

Quix BV Internship (2020.08-2020.10)

The third thing I did was an internship in the industry. I worked with Quix BV, a spin-off from University of Twente in the Netherlands that works on integrated photonics for quantum processors. It is a rapidly growing start-up that has great technology with Silicon-Nitride Waveguides[^5], and looking at potential new applications of their expertise. I was hired to investigate applications in QKD.

The internship was originally designed to incorporate computational works, lab works and designing of a first prototype of integrated photonics for QKD. This was disrupted by the COVID-19 pandemic. At one point it seemed unlikely the internship could go ahead. But after discussions with the company, we decided to change the internship into a remote one.

My work was almost like a consulting job. My employer asked me a hard question: Is their photonic technology useful for QKD? And I had 2 months to answer this question. I held weekly meetings with the company’s engineer to report my progress and discuss the directions of the project. In the end I compiled a 7000-word report based on my literature study on the topic “Integrated Photonics for Quantum Key Distribution”, in which I discussed past works on photonic-chip-based QKD within the scientific community, the technical requirements for building an on-chip QKD system, and most importantly, the report provided two practical routes for Quix to design their photonic chip in order to implement QKD.

Among the many things this project taught me, one was time management. The Quix internship overlapped with my PM QKD project. For Quix I was required to work a certain number of hours by my contract. So I budgeted my time, worked in some evenings and on some Saturdays. In the end, I made good progress on both projects, and managed to take enough rest over the summer as well.

To be continued

This is probably enough about myself now. But this is by no means the end of my journey in quantum, which will definitely continue in my fourth year and hopefully beyond. And this blog series will certainly be part of it.
