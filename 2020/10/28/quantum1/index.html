Privacy has been a tricky question in human communications for thousands of years.

The oldest use of cryptography is believed to date back to some 4000 years ago in Egypt, where the inscriptions on the tomb of a nobleman were written in a number of unusual hieroglyphs ^1. A more well known historical example is probably by Julius Caesar, whose encoding is by substituting the letter by one that is three positions to the right in the alphabet. Xvh lw qhaw wlph brx zdqw wr vhqg vhfuhw phvvdjhv wr brxu iulhqg! ^2

The RSA public key encryption scheme

Much more sophisticated schemes have been developed since Julius Caesar’s era. Today for internet security the RSA encryption scheme is widely used. Named after the initials of its inventors, Rivest, Shamir and Adleman, the RSA scheme bases its security on the difficulty of factoring large numbers.

Quantum Attac

All of this seems excellent. Indeed, the 2048 bit RSA encryption scheme is widely implemented for today’s cybersecurity. And after more than 40 years since RSA was invented, the computational hardness assumption is still solid: even the best classical computers today are still unable to factor large integers efficiently.

But then comes the idea of quantum computers. In classical computers calculations are performed with bits that take value of 0 or 1. Quantum computers, however, compute with quantum bits, or qubits.

What is a Qubit?

Generically, a qubit is a two-state quantum mechanical system. The general state of the system is

where and are in general complex.

For example the spin of an electron can be a qubit. Quantum mechanics tell us the electron spin can be spin up, spin down, or superposition of both spin up and down. Similarly, a qubit can be 0, or 1, or a superposition of 0 and 1. This unleashes massive computing power: qubits are capable of performing simultaneous operations!

Of course, the exact operations of a quantum computer would not be so intuitive. Because if we actually performed simultaneous operations, the output register of the quantum computer would also be in a superposition of all possible results. If we try to make a measurement, then the system collapses into only one state and such a state reduction seems to remove all advantages over classical computers. This problem was solved by a number of scientists including Deutsch and Jozsa. And the first non-trivial quantum algorithm was developed by Peter Shor in 1994, which tackles the exact problem that is at the core of RSA encryption scheme: how to factor large integers.

The exact details of quantum computing and quantum algorithms are not a focus of this blog series, as I myself still understand little about them. An interested reader is referred to more advanced textbooks such as Nielsen and Chuang [^4]. But the basic idea is that Shor’s algorithm is a polynomial time integer factorisation algorithm.

This immediately undermines the security assumption of the RSA scheme, at least theoretically. A recent study showed it is possible to factor 2048 bit RSA integers in 8 hours with 20 million qubits ^5. In comparison, the quantum computer with which Google claimed quantum supremacy had 54 qubits ^6. So there is no imminent threat to people’s online credit card transactions yet, and a scalable quantum computer is not yet in sight in foreseeable future. Of course, we should never except technology to advance linearly. What is merely a theoretical possibility might become reality in 25 years time or sooner.

Unconditional Security and the One-Time-Pad

What kind of encryption is secure, even if the eavesdropper gets hold of the theoretically most powerful quantum computer ever? Unconditional security methods give the answer: If a cryptographic scheme is provably secure without making any assumptions about the computing power and resources, then it is said to provide unconditional security.

An elegant unconditionally secure scheme is the one-time-pad. This scheme is very intuitive: the sender and the receiver encodes and decodes the message with a key that is known to themselves only, and they discard this key after single use. Quantum Protec

The core of this elegant method is the assumption that the key is only known to the two legitimate parties of the communication, i.e. only Alice and Bob can know . This could be done by having Alice pass the key on a handkerchief to Bob and then Bob burn it after reading, or by having Alice whisper it into Bob’s ear etc. But non of this is satisfactory for a noisy communication over more than 1 meter! This is a problem of key distribution.

How can we achieve provably secure key distribution for the unconditionally secure one-time-pad? In Chinese we say “Only the person who tied a bell to the tiger’s neck can untie the bell” (解铃还须系铃人), and it is once again quantum physics, the very science that broke classical cryptography, that provided the answer. And the answer is Quantum Key Distribution, the main theme of this blog series.

Afternote

I should have finished this post a week ago, but it turned out 4th year is even more stressful than I thought. But now I have written it, all is good.

It was a really interesting experience writing this post. I read some papers I would never have read otherwise, e.g. the RSA paper. It took me a long time to realise that Alice has to send the message in Bob’s encryption key, not in her own encryption key, which wouldn’t make any sense at all.

I also stumbled across a 1997 MIT lecture notes on unconditional security ^7. It was shocking how the notes gave excellent explanation of the one-time-pad, without mentioning the word “quantum” for even a single time! It would simply be unimaginable in the 21st century (an era where the word “quantum” basically means more research funding).

But, I should not deviate too much from the topic. Next time (hopefully in a week’s time), I shall write about the no-cloning theorem, the BB84 protocol, and the brief history behind it (spoiler: there will be “quantum banknotes”).
