In this post, I will briefly summarise the history of QKD before BB84 protocol, and describe how the famous BB84 protocol works.

Brief history before BB84

This section is mostly taken out of Gilles Brassard’s personal review of the history of Quantum Cryptography [^1].

The very first idea of cryptography that uses quantum mechanics can be traced back to ‘Quantum Banknotes’, an idea of Stephen Wiesner and Charles Bennett in 1960s, when they were in grad school: They imagined making banknotes of quantum nature, such that they are impossible to clone (Discussion on this quantum no-cloning theory in later sections).

This idea was largely disregarded by the mainstream scientific community, until an afternoon in late October 1979. Bennett approached Brassard at a swimming pool with his idea of Quantum Banknotes. Within hours, as Brassard claimed in his personal review, they came up with ways to combine this bizarre idea with the public-key encription scheme (The RSA scheme described in the previous post is an example of this). The term ‘Quantum Cryptography’ was coined the first time in their 1983 conference paper [^2].

However, in these early ideas, quantum information was held captive in one place by photon polarisation. The authors did not realise until a few years later that, in Brassard’s own words, ‘God has meant photons to travel rather than to stay put!’. In 1983 they came up with the idea to transmit an arbitrarily long random secret key in a quantum channel. Eavesdropping causes disturbance and can be detected. And if there is eavesdropping, the key can be simply thrown away!

Thus the idea of Quantum Key Distribution (QKD) was born, and its official birth certificate is an one-page abstract submitted to the 1983 IEEE Symposium on Information Theory [^3]. Shortly after this, the first QKD protocol was published in paper in full at another IEEE conference in Bangalore, India. This paper gave the protocol its famous name: BB84, after the initials of the two authors, Bennett and Brassard, and the year it was published, 1984 [^4]. To this day, the BB84 protocol is still the most widely implemented and successfully demonstrated quantum crypptographic protocol.

The BB84 Protocol

The BB84 protocol relies on two key ideas: the one-time pad and the quantum no-cloning theorem. I will explain these two concepts first, before moving on to the actual protocol.

Next time

Hopefully this post has not obsured the elegant construction of the BB84 protocol. I realise this is a rather mathematical approach, but I have always found a mathematical approach to be the best approach in terms of clarity.

The construction is indeed elegant, and it seems intuitive that QKD is the safest cryptographic scheme theoretically possible. But intuition is not enough to rigorously prove its security. In the next post I aim to give an overview of security analyses of QKD.

Additionally, proving the security of QKD under ideal conditions is one thing, achieving practical security with realistic imperfect devices is another. Practical security will be another topic in future posts of this blog series.
